Amherstburg proposes transformative $50M project

Author of the article:
Anne Jarvis  •  Windsor Star
Publishing date:
Oct 29, 2019  •  October 29, 2019  •  3 minute read  •  Join the conversation

Article content

Amherstburg is proposing an ambitious and transformative $50-million project for the former Duffy’s Motor Inn and Tavern land, Belle Vue and the Libro Centre

The town’s council voted Monday to apply for grants from the federal and provincial governments for the project under the Investing in Canada Infrastructure Program. If approved, the federal government would pay 40 per cent of the cost, the provincial government 33 per cent and the town 26.67 per cent or $13.3 million.

The most costly part of the project would be $25.29 million to create a “centre of excellence” at the Libro Centre. That project would include a fully accessible indoor 25-metre pool, a “hub” of three baseball diamonds, plus soccer pitches, more pickle ball courts, a fully accessible playground, a Big Creek trail and lookout, pavilion with change rooms and event plaza. A skateboard park would also be relocated there.

It would be stupid not to put our town forward

The Big Creek trail “would be taking advantage of one of our most prized amenities,” said CAO John Miceli, who presented the plan to council. “We’re sitting on one of the most underutilised green spaces in the province.”

The trail would be, he promised, “like no other in this region.”

The plan would also include outdoor skating.

The Libro Centre is surrounded by almost 80 acres — “a tremendous amount,” Miceli said — of undeveloped land.

The Waterfront Civic Plaza would cost $11.5 million and include a civic square and extended Kings Navy Yard Park, amphitheatre, accessible multi-use pier for everything from fishing to tall ships, marina and accessible walking path with a bench and water and fire feature.

Belle Vue Gardens and Conservatory, to be created around the historic house that the town bought in 2016, would cost $10.34 million and include botanical gardens and a Carolinian arboretum, greenhouses, the Historical Walking Mile connecting to Fort Malden, a pavilion and community venue.

A plan called The Big Connection, connecting the three sites, would cost another $2.87 million and include improvements to the Simcoe Road multi-use trail, making the road a “complete street” and prioritizing people over vehicles, and development of “community connectors” to link existing hubs or areas of interest as well as developing links among neighbourhoods.

The project would unfold over seven years.

Residents have told the town they want more access to the Detroit River and more waterfront amenities and more outdoor recreation facilities, Miceli said.

The project would check off 48 of the 77 recommendations in the parks master plan, 32 of which are considered high priority, Miceli said.

“We’re talking about 27-cent dollars to address our wish list,” he said. “Without senior governments, this would be very, very difficult. This is an opportunity.”

The town’s share would come from long-term debt, reserves and tax levies. The town could also fundraise, sell other assets or search for “efficiencies,” Miceli said.

Deputy Mayor Leo Meloche warned about “hidden costs” such as operating costs and maintenance and repair costs that come with such ambitious projects.

But council agreed it should at least apply for the senior government money.

“It would be stupid not to put our town forward,” said Coun. Peter Courtney.

“One overriding comment I heard was get ‘er done,” McArthur said of the Duffy’s property, which the town bought two years ago.

“This is a warranted expense,” one resident told council. “I hope the town is successful with this application.”
